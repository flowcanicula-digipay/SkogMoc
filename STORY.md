why the business is shaped the way it is, and the voice every page should
share. If a piece of copy contradicts this file, the copy is wrong, not

## Who Skog Mộc Is

Skog Mộc by TNP is a **general architecture and design studio** based in
Biên Hòa, Đồng Nai. It does the full range of work a residential
architecture practice does for Vietnamese homes: concept design,
construction documentation, renovations, additions, interior design, and
landscape design. That is the business — broad, general, capable of
carrying a project from an empty plot or an aging house through to a
finished home.

**Within that general practice, furniture is the studio's standout
strength.** Skog Mộc designs, makes, and installs furniture better than
anyone else it competes with — this is not a side offering bolted onto an
architecture firm, and it is not the entry point a new client is funneled
toward. It is the thing the studio is unbeaten at, and the moment it
matters most is specific and recurring:

- **Once a plan is already built.** A house or renovation has a finished
  architectural plan — drawn by Skog Mộc or, just as often, by someone
  else entirely. The plan defines the rooms; it doesn't finish them.
  Furniture is the step that turns a built plan into a place someone
  actually lives — and it's the step where Skog Mộc has no equal.
- **Whenever a renovation is planned.** A client doesn't always need (or
  want) a new architectural scheme. Often the layout is fine and what's
  needed is new furniture for a refreshed room, a converted space, or a
  renovation already underway with someone else's builder. Skog Mộc plugs
  in at exactly that stage, no full architectural re-engagement required.

Put plainly: **architecture is what Skog Mộc is. Furniture is what Skog
Mộc is best at.** Marketing should make the studio's range obvious (it's a
real architecture practice, not a furniture shop with an architecture
page) while still making sure nobody leaves the site unsure that furniture
is the area where the studio simply cannot be matched.

## Why This Distinction Matters

A "furniture-first" story (the prior revision) implies the studio is
fundamentally a furniture business that happens to also offer design
services — which undersells the practice's actual range and miscasts
furniture as a beginner-friendly upsell rather than a hard-won, unmatched
specialty. It also weakens the studio's credibility with renovation and
new-build clients who need a real architecture partner, not a furniture
vendor.

An "architecture-led, furniture-is-our-edge" story does three things the
prior framing didn't:

1. **Establishes credibility first.** A client researching an architecture
   studio for a renovation or a new house sees a general practice capable
   of the whole job — not a furniture catalogue.
2. **Makes the furniture claim land harder.** "Nobody does this better
   than us" is a much stronger claim from a studio that obviously does
   everything else competently too. The claim of being unmatched only
   means something next to genuine general capability.
3. **Matches how the work actually happens.** Furniture isn't the first
   conversation in most projects — it's the step that happens once a plan
   exists, whether that plan is Skog Mộc's own design or a renovation
   already in motion. The story should follow that sequence, not invert it.

## Messaging Pillars

1. **General architecture practice.** Concept design, construction
   documentation, renovations, and additions for Vietnamese homes — tube
   houses, multi-generational households, tropical-climate detailing.
   This is the studio's identity and should read first on every page that
   introduces the company (hero, About, meta descriptions).
2. **Furniture: the specialty nobody else matches.** Design, fabrication,
   and installation, delivered once a plan is in place — whether that
   plan came from Skog Mộc or from somewhere else. This is the studio's
   sharpest edge and its strongest proof point (most of the real
   portfolio, today, is furniture work) — but it is presented as the
   studio's best work, not its whole business.
3. **Interior and landscape design, as part of one practice.** Not
   "secondary," not an upsell — integral disciplines inside a general
   architecture practice, brought in as a project needs them.
4. **The extended specialist network.** Architectural technologist,
   interior & spatial designer, building surveyor, town planner, production
   designer, historic buildings inspector, structural engineer — available
   through the studio's network when a project's technical or regulatory
   needs go beyond the core team. This part of the story is unchanged from
   the prior revision.

## Voice Guidance for Copy

- Lead with architecture/general-practice language in any section that
  introduces the company cold (hero title, About section, meta titles and
  descriptions, the services page hero). A first-time visitor should
  understand "this is an architecture studio" before they understand
  "and they're incredible at furniture."
- Once that's established, let furniture be the specific, confident claim:
  "nobody does this better," "where we're unmatched," "the part of the
  process we don't lose at" — not hedged, not apologetic.
- Tie furniture explicitly to the two real triggers — *a plan that's
  already built* and *a renovation that's already planned* — rather than
  presenting it as a generic standalone product. The phrase "once your
  plan is in place" (or equivalent) should recur.
- The portfolio is allowed to be (and currently is) mostly furniture work.
  That's a fact about the studio's track record, not a contradiction of
  "general architecture practice" — frame it as "this is what we're best
  known for," not as evidence the studio only does furniture.
- Call it a **portfolio**, not "case studies" — the studio has real
  completed projects but isn't publishing detailed case-study write-ups
  (specific addresses, client names, budgets) yet. Real, confirmed facts
  that are safe to state plainly: the work is houses, villas, and
  penthouses, located in Vinhomes Golden River, Vinhomes Central Park,
  Thủ Thiêm, Thảo Điền, and Phú Mỹ Hưng (all in Ho Chi Minh City). Don't
  go further than that — no invented project specifics — until the owner
  supplies real photography and case-by-case detail (see CLAUDE.md →
  "Portfolio / Seed Projects").
- Don't resurrect "furniture made for how you live" as the tagline framing
  — that phrase puts furniture in the company-identity slot. The tagline
  should put architecture/the general practice in that slot and furniture
  in the differentiator slot.
